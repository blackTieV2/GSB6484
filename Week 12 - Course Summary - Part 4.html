Part 4: How Do We Behave Ethically Within an Organization? (Weeks 9-11)

Key Learning Objectives

Upon completing this part, students will be able to:

  • Understand the principles of ethical leadership and the role of leaders in fostering an ethical culture within organizations.
  • Analyze the importance of corporate governance and risk management in promoting ethical behavior.
  • Evaluate the mechanisms for implementing and institutionalizing ethics within organizations.
  • Discuss the ethical obligations of professionals to society and the challenges of balancing self-interest with moral obligations.

Main Topics

Ethical Leadership

Ethical leadership is essential for setting the moral tone of an organization. Leaders must demonstrate integrity, transparency, and a commitment to ethical values to inspire and influence their followers. Ethical leaders act proactively towards influencing ethical and unethical behavior within the organization.

  • Leader Morality: Ethical leaders prioritize ethics in decision-making and ensure their own behavior is morally sound.
  • Follower Morality: Ethical leaders need ethical followers who support and uphold the organization's ethical standards.

Corporate Governance and Risk Management

Corporate governance involves the systems and processes by which organizations are directed and controlled. Effective governance ensures accountability, transparency, and ethical behavior. Risk management is integral to corporate governance, as it involves identifying, assessing, and prioritizing risks to minimize negative impacts on the organization and its stakeholders.

  • Board of Directors: The board is responsible for representing shareholder interests and directing management to achieve ethical and sustainable business practices.
  • Obligations of Directors: Directors must exercise their powers with care, diligence, and in good faith, acting in the best interests of the company and its stakeholders.

Institutionalizing Ethics

Institutionalizing ethics involves embedding ethical principles and standards into the organizational culture and practices. This can be achieved through the development and implementation of ethics programs, codes of conduct, and policies that promote ethical behavior and decision-making.

  • Ethics Program: A comprehensive ethics program includes a code of ethics, policies for investigating and disciplining unethical behavior, incentives for ethical behavior, ethics training, an ethics helpline, monitoring systems, and ethics audits.
  • Education, Training, and Commitment: Continuous education and training are essential to keep the ethics program dynamic and relevant. Commitment from all levels of the organization is crucial for the successful implementation of ethical standards.
  • Auditing an Ethics System: Regular audits are necessary to ensure the ethics system is functioning as intended. The audit must be evidence-based, rigorous, and conducted by independent auditors to avoid conflicts of interest.

Balancing Self-Interest and Moral Obligations

Balancing self-interest with moral obligations is a critical aspect of ethical decision-making. Professionals have a duty to act in the best interests of their stakeholders and society while considering their own self-interest. This balance is particularly relevant in situations involving conflicts of interest, whistleblowing, and corporate governance lapses.

Critical Theories and Frameworks

The study of ethical behavior within organizations involves various critical theories and frameworks that provide different perspectives on leadership, governance, and ethical practices. These include:

Kohlberg’s Stages of Moral Development

Kohlberg’s theory of moral development outlines three levels of moral reasoning: pre-conventional, conventional, and post-conventional. Each level represents a different approach to ethical decision-making, ranging from obedience to rules to principled reasoning based on universal ethical principles.

Corporate Governance Frameworks

Various frameworks guide corporate governance practices, including the COSO Enterprise Risk Management (ERM) framework, which provides a structured approach to managing risks. The COSO framework consists of five levels: governance and culture, strategy and objective-setting, performance, review and revision, and information, communication, and reporting.

  • Governance & Culture: Exercises board risk oversight, establishes operating structures, defines desired culture, demonstrates commitment to core values, and attracts, develops, and retains capable individuals.
  • Strategy & Objective-Setting: Analyzes business context, defines risk appetite, evaluates alternative strategies, and formulates business objectives.
  • Performance: Identifies risks, assesses severity of risks, prioritizes risks, implements risk responses, and develops portfolio view.
  • Review & Revision: Assesses substantial change, reviews risk and performance, and pursues improvement in enterprise risk management.
  • Information, Communication, & Reporting: Leverages information and technology, communicates risk information, and reports on risk, culture, and performance.

Normative Ethics

Normative ethics focuses on creating or stating rules of behavior that standardize, control, regulate, and prescribe ethical conduct. This approach sets values and norms that guide behavior, emphasizing what is morally right or wrong, good or bad, just or unjust.

  • Justice and Fairness: Ensuring fair treatment and equitable distribution of benefits and burdens.
  • Integrity and Honesty: Upholding truthfulness and transparency in all business dealings.

Definitions of Technical Terms

  • Ethical Leadership: The practice of setting and promoting ethical standards and behavior within an organization.
  • Corporate Governance: The systems and processes by which organizations are directed and controlled to ensure accountability, transparency, and ethical behavior.
  • Risk Management: The process of identifying, assessing, and prioritizing risks to minimize negative impacts on the organization and its stakeholders.
  • Ethics Program: A comprehensive system that includes a code of ethics, policies, training, and monitoring mechanisms to promote ethical behavior within an organization.
  • Conflict of Interest: A situation in which an individual’s personal interests could interfere with their professional responsibilities and decision-making.
